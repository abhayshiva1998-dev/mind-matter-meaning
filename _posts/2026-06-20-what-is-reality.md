## Introduction

Reality is not simply whatever happens to be visible. A mirage appears, a dream is experienced, and a mathematical proof can be understood without becoming a physical object. The question is how these cases fit into a disciplined account of what exists.

## Main Discussion

A useful starting point separates appearance from structure. Appearance is what an observer encounters. Structure is what remains stable across reliable inquiry. Science studies structure through measurement, prediction, and correction. Philosophy asks what must be true for such inquiry to make sense.

Mathematics gives a compact example. The expression $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ is not a sensory object, but it describes a relation that can govern physical measurement. Display equations should render as well:

$$
E = mc^2
$$

Reality may therefore include physical things, formal relations, meanings, and conscious appearances, while assigning each a different mode of dependence.
